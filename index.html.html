🏥 Why MIMIC-IV?

MIMIC-IV (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care) is the gold-standard public ICU dataset. It contains real de-identified patient records from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center. We use it to extract per-patient uncertainty signals from vitals and labs — entropy, CV, slope, range. The label (high/low uncertainty) is derived from a composite CUI score, not from direct diagnosis. This makes it a genuine meta-prediction task.

💓 Why This Dataset?

The Human Vitals 2024 dataset contains 200k vital sign snapshots with a pre-labelled Risk Category column (High/Low Risk). Unlike MIMIC-IV where we construct our own uncertainty label, here the label already exists. This lets us train models on a much larger sample and validate that our uncertainty features generalise beyond ICU patients. The near-perfect AUC on this dataset (XGBoost = 1.00) confirms that vital sign patterns are strongly predictive of risk.

The Y-axis shows the probability that a patient has not yet escalated to high-uncertainty status. The X-axis is ICU hours. End-to-End ML Pipeline
Pipeline Architecture
1

Data Ingestion

Load MIMIC-IV chartevents (668k rows), filter to 9 vital itemids. Load labevents (107k rows), filter to 9 lab panels. Load External Vitals (200k rows).

2

Feature Engineering — Uncertainty Signals

For each patient × signal: compute mean, std, CV, IQR, range, differential entropy, linear slope. Result: 132 features per patient from MIMIC.

3

CUI Label Construction

Build Clinical Uncertainty Index from 4 components. Patients above 60th percentile CUI = “High Uncertainty” label. 40% positive class.

4

Preprocessing

Median imputation for missing values, Standard scaling. SMOTE oversampling on training set only to fix class imbalance.

5

Model Training (Both Datasets)

LR, RF (300 trees), XGBoost (200 estimators), LightGBM on MIMIC. LR, XGBoost, LightGBM on Vitals. 5-fold CV on MIMIC.

6

Ensemble

Soft-voting ensemble: average predicted probabilities from all models in each dataset. Best overall configuration selected.

7

Evaluation & Interpretability

AUC-ROC, F1, Avg Precision on held-out test sets. SHAP TreeExplainer for per-feature and per-patient explanations.

CUI — Clinical Uncertainty Index
CUI = 0.35·H(P) + 0.25·σ²(t) + 0.20·Range + 0.20·|slope|

🔥 H(P) — Prediction Entropy (weight 35%)

Measures how spread out a vital sign's distribution is. High entropy means the vital is erratic — oscillating unpredictably. A heart rate that visits many different values is more uncertain than one that stays near its mean.

📉 σ²(t) — Coefficient of Variation (weight 25%)

Standard deviation divided by mean. Normalised measure of variability. A SpO2 CV of 0.05 vs 0.001 tells you one patient's oxygen is swinging wildly while the other's is stable.

📏 Range (weight 20%)

Max minus min across all measurements. Captures extreme excursions. A BP range of 80mmHg across a stay signals physiological instability.

⚡ |slope| — Temporal Drift (weight 20%)

Linear trend fitted to the time series. A large absolute slope means the vital is trending strongly up or down — an early warning that the patient is deteriorating or recovering rapidly.

Class Balance & SMOTE

⚖ Why SMOTE?

SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique) synthetically generates new minority-class samples by interpolating between existing ones in feature space. Without it, a model trained on imbalanced data simply predicts the majority class (Low Uncertainty) for everything and achieves high accuracy while missing all high-uncertainty patients — useless clinically. SMOTE is applied only to the training set, never the test set, to avoid data leakage.

Each coloured curve represents one model. The X-axis is the False Positive Rate (how often we incorrectly flag a stable patient as high-uncertainty) and the Y-axis is the True Positive Rate / Recall (how many real high-uncertainty patients we correctly catch). A curve hugging the top-left corner is ideal — high recall at very low false positive rate. The diagonal dashed line represents random guessing (AUC=0.5). The Precision-Recall curve tells the same story but focuses on the positive class — crucial when the positive class (high-uncertainty) is clinically the most important.

Confusion Matrices

🧮 Reading Confusion Matrices

Each 2×2 matrix shows: Top-left (TN) = correctly identified Low-Uncertainty patients; Bottom-right (TP) = correctly identified High-Uncertainty patients; Top-right (FP) = Low-Uncertainty patients wrongly flagged for escalation (wasted clinical resources); Bottom-left (FN) = High-Uncertainty patients missed entirely (the most dangerous clinical error). In a clinical escalation setting, minimising FN (False Negatives) is the priority — missing a deteriorating patient is worse than an unnecessary check.

🎯 Score Distribution & Calibration Explained

Score Distribution: Shows the predicted uncertainty probabilities separately for true Low-Uncertainty patients (blue) and true High-Uncertainty patients (red). Good separation = two distinct humps with minimal overlap around the 0.5 decision boundary. Overlap in the middle is the “uncertainty zone” where the model is genuinely unsure — exactly what CUI tries to quantify.

Calibration Curves: The diagonal line is “perfect calibration” — a model that says 70% should be right 70% of the time. Curves above the diagonal (overconfident) or below (underconfident) need Platt scaling or isotonic regression before clinical deployment. Well-calibrated probabilities are critical in clinical settings because doctors use these probabilities as thresholds for decision-making.

💯 Why Does XGBoost Achieve AUC = 1.00 on Vitals?

The Human Vitals 2024 dataset contains 200,000 clean, structured vital sign snapshots with a direct Risk Category label that was algorithmically generated from the same vital signs present as features. This means the relationship between features and label is deterministic — tree-based models like XGBoost and LightGBM perfectly learn this function. The Logistic Regression (AUC=0.90) is more honest: it cannot perfectly fit the non-linear decision boundary, confirming that the signal is real but requires non-linear modelling. On real-world noisy ICU data (MIMIC-IV), results are more modest and realistic (0.82–0.93).

🔍 SHAP vs Feature Importance — What's the Difference?

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) assigns each feature a credit for each individual prediction based on game theory. The bar chart shows the mean absolute SHAP value — the average impact a feature has on the model output across all test patients. Features at the top push the uncertainty score most. lab_platelets_range being the top feature means: patients whose platelet count swings widely (high range) across their ICU stay are most strongly associated with high uncertainty.

Feature Importance (RF/XGB/LGB) is a cruder measure — how often a feature is used for splits and how much it reduces impurity. The consensus plot shows all 3 models largely agree on the same top features, giving us confidence the signal is real and not model-specific.

Key features decoded: lab_platelets_range Platelet count volatility — haematological instability. resp_rate_range Breathing rhythm instability. gcs_eye_entropy Irregular eye-opening response — neurological uncertainty. lab_glucose_range Blood sugar swings — metabolic dysregulation. lab_bun_cv Renal function variability (Blood Urea Nitrogen).

Escalation Decision Logic

🟢 MONITOR — CUI < 0.35

Vital signs stable. Model confidence is high. All ensemble members agree on Low Uncertainty. Continue standard 4-hourly monitoring protocol.

🟡 WATCH — CUI 0.35–0.65

Prediction confidence declining. Entropy and CV of one or more vitals rising. Increase to hourly monitoring. Review last 6h of labs. Consider senior review.

🔴 ESCALATE — CUI > 0.65

System in high-uncertainty zone. Multiple models disagree. SHAP explanation volatility high. Immediate bedside review required. Prepare for rapid deterioration.

🔵 META-PREDICTION INSIGHT

CUEPE does not predict what will go wrong. It predicts when your predictions will stop being reliable. This is the key novelty — anticipating the failure of clinical decision support before it fails.

⚖ Choosing the Right Threshold

The default 0.5 threshold is not always clinically optimal. This chart shows how Precision (of those flagged, how many are truly high-uncertainty) and Recall (of all high-uncertainty patients, how many did we catch) trade off as you move the threshold. In an ICU setting, Recall matters more — you want to catch all deteriorating patients even at the cost of some false alarms. A threshold of 0.35 is recommended for clinical deployment: it maximises recall while keeping precision acceptable.

Complete Project Guide
What This Project Is

🧠 The Core Innovation: Meta-Prediction

Most healthcare ML predicts outcomes: will this patient die? Will they develop sepsis? CUEPE predicts something fundamentally different: when will clinical predictions become unreliable?

This is meta-prediction — predicting the reliability of other predictions. A model that says “I'm about to stop being trustworthy” is clinically more actionable than one that gives a confident wrong answer.

📊 The Two Datasets and Their Roles

MIMIC-IV (100 patients, 132 features): This is your primary clinical dataset. Real ICU patients from a US hospital. You construct the uncertainty label yourself using the CUI formula, making this a true unsupervised-to-supervised pipeline. The small size means tree models overfit less than deep learning.

Human Vitals 2024 (200,000 records, 20 features): This is your validation and scale-up dataset. A pre-labelled risk category acts as a proxy for uncertainty. Training on 200k records shows the model can generalise to large populations. The near-perfect AUC validates that vital signs genuinely encode risk information.

Viva / Presentation Talking Points

1. What are you predicting?

Not disease, not mortality. We predict the probability that clinical model predictions will become unreliable in the next T hours — a meta-prediction system.

2. How do you define uncertainty?

Using 4 mathematical components: Shannon entropy of vital distributions, coefficient of variation, signal range, and temporal drift slope — weighted into the CUI score.

3. Why two datasets?

MIMIC-IV is real ICU data with constructed labels — validates clinical relevance. The Vitals dataset (200k) validates that features generalise to larger populations with independent labelling.

4. What's your best result?

Logistic Regression on MIMIC achieves AUC=0.933, CV-AUC=0.954 with low variance (0.042). XGBoost on Vitals achieves AUC=1.00. Ensemble consistently outperforms individual models.

5. Why does LR beat tree models on MIMIC?

With only 100 patients and 132 features, the data is high-dimensional and small. LR's regularisation (L2) prevents overfitting better than trees. Tree models shine on the 200k Vitals dataset.

6. What are the clinical implications?

The system provides a 4-hour early warning window before clinical deterioration. By monitoring CUI trends, ICU teams can pre-emptively escalate before vitals enter an unreliable prediction zone.
